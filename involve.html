Brief

"Inclusive design doesn't mean you're designing one thing for all people. You're designing a diversity of ways to participate so that everyone has a sense of belonging."

- Susan Goltsman

Adobe partnered with Amazon to host a designathon focused on inclusive design and accessibility. With over 350 students that participated, I placed in the 10th percentile. This was my first designathon and so going into this event I wanted to just have fun with it and see what I can create.

The Prompt

Inspired by Amazon's initiative, your challenge is to design an inclusive experience in a third-party mobile app for high school students looking for opportunities to fulfill community service hours or volunteer to help their local community, no matter what their background or ability.

Reflecting on when I was a highschool student looking for volunteer hours, I remember that I only volunteered at places where I was familiar with. With limited experiences as a high school student, the discoverability of volunteer roles are less prominent than a job search.

How Might We??

When coming up with some goals and picturing the target audience, I came up with these how might we questions to help brainstorm ideas.

Use Cases + Goals

Having two brothers in high school also helped with imagining the target user and getting more insights to the needs of this user group. Utilising their network of high school students, I was able to collect information to curate a typical persona for this product.

Considering all user types

As I was coming up with a general user group that would be targetted, I also considered the accessibility needs of people with disabilities that may want to use the app as well. I created a chart that outlines all the use cases that I could think of along with their accessibility needs.

Site Map + Features

Because of the time limitation, it was crucial to showcase only the key features and functionalities of the product. I ended up designing 5 different functionalities for the app. These functionalities included:
  • Being suggested volunteer positions based on location/interest
  • View and manage scheduling of volunteer roles
  • Search for volunteer roles
  • Track hours and customize accessibility settings
  • View application progress of applied roles on the app
Once I figured out the main functionalities of the app, I created a site map to guide myself in designing the flow of the whole app and making sure I made screens for each map component.

Design Solution

Mentorship
As a part of the creative jam, participants were able to get a mentorship session with an Amazon designer. I got the chance to get feedback on my designs with one of Amazon's Principal designer, Michele Palazzo. Some of the feedback I took from the session included:
  • Have tiles so that users can direct attention to one position at a time
  • Adjust search engine to be selective rather than have a type functionality
  • Reduce and simplify text to avoid screens being too text heavy
  • Reduce onboarding process to only what is considered necessary information

Onboarding

I aimed to make the onboarding process as quick and efficient as possible.

Instead of having to input their information manually, students can have their ID scanned to retrieve basic information used to track volunteer hours.

Following that, they can then set their interests and customize accessibility settings to their needs.

Explore

The explore page acts as the main page for the app.

Browsing

Students can browse through volunteer positions based on the interests they've selected or by location.

The tiles displayed only the most important information which includes:

  • Title
  • Company
  • Volunteer date
  • Number of hours

Save

If they find interest in a position, they can save the position and it will show up int he 'Saved' tab.

Search Engine

If users want to search for more volunteer roles, they can tap the search button at the top of the explore page. This will lead the user to a couple of search filters that will help curate a list of search results for them.

To make it easier to filter through the jobs, I added filter and sort functions to help with refining search.

Schedule

After speaking with some highschool students about their experiences with volunteering, there was a common need for students to keep track and be reminded of their day-to-day tasks.

"School oftentimes gets busy, it would be nice to have some sort of schedule that reminds me of the things I have to do"

Inbox

The inbox was a place for students to keep track of their application progress. They can see what positions they've applied for, which jobs have been approved and look at their past history of jobs.

The inbox also connects to the scheduling page by allowing students to add successful jobs into their calendar.

Profile
The profile page contains the user's personal information, volunteer hour tracking, and settings. I planned on incorporating a gamification process to the app by utilising awards/badges as a motivator for students to volunteer more. Unfortunately, I wasn't able to fully flush out the concept in time in which I would have made it a more prominent feature of the app.
How I incorporated inclusive design
Since inclusive design was the main focus of the prompt, here was how I believed I achieved that in my design:

Including accessibility into onboarding

Including accessibility options in the onboarding process adds consideration of all user types before even interacting with the app.

Adding the option for text-to-speech

In each job posting, I've included an audio option for any user to be able convert the text-speech by simply tapping the button

Filtering options

Each volunteer position require different skillsets. This makes it important to have a filtering function to be able to filter based on difficulty, availability, and environment (in-person/remote) considering students have different preferences.

Takeaways

With the continuation of online school, I wanted to keep myself busy and challenge myself to get creative and be inspired by other student's work. This experience was super fun and a learning opportunity. Here are my final thoughts:
Designing with everyone in mind

The importance of practicing inclusive design and prioritizing accessibility is evidently growing. As UX designers, we should continue to strive to design for all and make sure that our designs abide with accessibility standards.

Continuing to iterate and grow as a designer

Designs are never completely done and closed, and if I had all the time in the world I would be constantly looking for ways to improve and iterate on my design. I think it is really insightful to be able to see other participant's work and understand their thought processes. In doing so, I feel like I have gotten newfound inspiration to take with me into my next project!

It is not so often that we get the chance to practice designing and have complete creative freedom. This experience was a nice opportunity to continue to develop my skills as a designer and be more involved in the design community

The Adobe Creative Jam hosts so many events throughout the year that I am already looking forward to see what I can accomplish in the next event! Stay tuned!
